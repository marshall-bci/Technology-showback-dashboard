🎨 Design Rationale — CTO view vs Admin vs Stakeholder

  • Dark command strip at the top: A CTO opens this at 8am and needs 4 numbers in under 5 seconds — total spend, showback coverage, unrecovered amount, and data health. The dark strip with colour-coded values (green/amber/red) gives the status at a glance before they read a single word. vs Admin Admin sees item counts and operational flags. CTO sees programme-level metrics.
  • Showback coverage % is the headline metric, not total spend: The CTO's job is to maximise the showback coverage rate — that is the strategic goal of the programme. 74.8% is the number that tells them whether the programme is working. Total spend ($15.5M) is secondary — it's context, not the outcome. vs Stakeholder A stakeholder cares about their bill. The CTO cares about the programme coverage rate.
  • $3.9M unrecovered is a decision card, not a hidden fact: In the admin and stakeholder views, this gap is either buried or invisible. For the CTO it is the most important call-to-action — they need to approve which items move to headcount model, which stay as spread allocation, and which are below the materiality threshold. The gap card surfaces this prominently with a breakdown of what to do with each slice.
  • LOB table shows the methods per LOB (coloured dots), not just amounts: A CTO needs to understand which LOBs are on which model, not just their dollar amount. The coloured dots (headcount/consumption/chargeback) let them see at a glance that ISR is headcount-only and may be a candidate for a consumption model, while Finance uses all three. vs Stakeholder Stakeholders see their own department only. CTO sees all 12 in one ranked view.
  • "Decisions Needed" section at the bottom: Unlike the stakeholder view (which has no actions) and the admin view (which has operational flags), the CTO view ends with three decision cards — each with a recommended action and ownership. These are strategic/governance decisions, not operational fixes. The CTO should be able to read this section and write three emails.
  • 4-year cost trajectory instead of a donut: The CTO is accountable to the board for technology cost trajectory. FY2025 → FY2026 → FY2027 → FY2028 in a single row answers "are we growing sustainably?" and "are we on track against the approved budget ceiling?" The donut chart (showback type split) is operational detail, not executive information.
  • Data quality is framed as a governance risk, not an operational task: 111 flags means 49.8% of allocations are best-guess. A CTO needs to know this not to fix it themselves, but to assign it as a task with accountability. The decision card frames it as "Assign to Finance team" — a management action, not a data entry problem.
